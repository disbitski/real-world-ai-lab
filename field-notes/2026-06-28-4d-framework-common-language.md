# Field Note: The 4D Framework Is A Common Language For AI Collaboration

Date: 2026-06-28

## Summary

Over the last couple of days, I learned Anthropic's AI Fluency 4D framework:
Description, Delegation, Discernment, and Diligence. The more I sit with it,
the more useful it feels as shared language for explaining the shift we are
living through with AI.

My interpretation is that the 4Ds do for human-AI collaboration what Six Sigma
did for process improvement in many companies: they give people a common,
consistent vocabulary for work that can otherwise feel abstract, emotional, or
too technical to discuss clearly.

In my early career at Johnson & Johnson, Green Belt training helped me see how
powerful a shared operating language can be. Six Sigma gave people words,
steps, measurements, and expectations for improving work together. I think the
4D framework can play a similar role for AI collaboration: not as a rigid
corporate program, but as a practical language for deciding what humans should
own, what AI can help with, how we judge outputs, and what responsibility we
retain.

## Observation

The Anthropic AI Fluency materials describe four connected competencies:

- Description: explaining goals, context, process, output, format, audience,
  style, and desired behavior clearly enough for AI to help.
- Delegation: deciding what I should do, what AI should do, and what we should
  do together.
- Discernment: evaluating what AI produces, how it got there, and whether the
  interaction itself is working.
- Diligence: taking responsibility for the systems I use, how transparent I am
  about AI's role, and what I verify before sharing or using an output.

That framework immediately helped me in a real conversation outside tech. I was
talking with a local business owner about AI, and instead of saying vague
things like "AI will change everything," I could talk about Delegation and
Discernment in normal language.

Delegation made the conversation less threatening: what are humans good at, what
is AI good at, and where is the combo stronger than either one alone?

Discernment made the conversation more trustworthy: AI outputs still need to be
checked, especially when they become artifacts other people will rely on.

That is where it clicked for me. The language helped move the conversation away
from "AI replaces people" and toward "humans and AI need a better collaboration
model."

## Why It Matters

New technology adoption often fails because people do not share the same words
for what is changing. One person says "automation" and means cost cutting.
Another says "AI assistant" and means a drafting partner. Another hears
"agent" and imagines fully autonomous work with no review.

The 4Ds lower that confusion.

They let me explain AI collaboration as a set of human skills:

| Competency | The plain-language question |
| --- | --- |
| Description | Can I explain what good looks like? |
| Delegation | Can I decide the right split between me, AI, and us together? |
| Discernment | Can I judge whether the output and process are good enough? |
| Diligence | Can I take responsibility for how AI is used and disclosed? |

That framing is useful because it keeps people in the work. AI is not just a
replacement machine, and it is not just a chatbot. It is a collaboration layer
that becomes more valuable when humans get better at describing, delegating,
checking, and taking responsibility.

## My Six Sigma Analogy

ASQ describes Six Sigma as a disciplined, data-driven approach to improving
process capability, reducing variation, and using defined methods such as DMAIC:
define, measure, analyze, improve, and control.

The important part for me is not that AI fluency should become Six Sigma. It is
that Six Sigma showed me the power of common language.

Green Belt training gave people a way to talk about work as a process. It made
improvement more measurable, more teachable, and less dependent on one person's
opinion. When a team could say "define the problem," "measure the baseline," or
"control the process," the conversation had rails.

The 4D framework gives AI conversations similar rails:

- Description helps me define the work before asking AI to help.
- Delegation helps me choose the right human/AI split.
- Discernment helps me inspect quality, reasoning, fit, and behavior.
- Diligence helps me own the consequences of using AI-generated work.

That is the practical bridge: common language turns fuzzy change into something
people can discuss, teach, evaluate, and improve.

## A Simple Way I Can Explain The 4Ds

When I talk with someone who is not deep in AI, I do not need to start with
models, tokens, agents, context windows, or tooling.

I can start here:

```text
Description: Tell AI what good looks like.
Delegation: Decide what you do, what AI does, and what you do together.
Discernment: Check the work before you trust it.
Diligence: Be responsible for how you use it and what you share.
```

That is enough to make the conversation concrete.

From there, I can give examples:

- A business owner can use AI to draft a customer email, but still own the
  customer relationship and final message.
- A manager can use AI to summarize reviews, but still check tone, accuracy,
  fairness, and missing context.
- A builder can use AI to generate code, but still run tests, inspect diffs,
  and own deployment.
- A creator can use AI to produce options, but still own taste, intent, and
  attribution.

The point is not to make AI sound smaller. The point is to make the
collaboration model clearer.

## Evaluation Ideas

I can evaluate whether an AI workflow demonstrates the 4Ds with questions like:

- Did I describe the goal, audience, constraints, and desired output clearly?
- Did I consciously decide what I would do, what AI would do, and what we would
  do together?
- Did I check the output for accuracy, appropriateness, coherence, relevance,
  and process quality?
- Did I disclose or document AI's role when the context required transparency?
- Did I keep responsibility for consequential decisions instead of outsourcing
  judgment to the model?
- Did the shared language help another person understand AI collaboration more
  clearly?

## Working Principle

The 4D framework gives AI collaboration a common language: describe the work,
delegate intentionally, discern the output, and stay diligent about human
responsibility.
