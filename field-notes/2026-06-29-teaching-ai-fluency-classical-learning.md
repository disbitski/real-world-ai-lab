# Field Note: Teaching AI Fluency Feels Like Classical Learning For The AI Era

Date: 2026-06-29

## Summary

Anthropic's Teaching AI Fluency course landed for me because it was not really
about teaching people how to prompt. It was about teaching people how to think,
judge, collaborate, explain, reflect, and take responsibility in a world where
AI can do more of the procedural work.

That has been sitting with me because I have two daughters in college right
now. Education is not abstract in my house. I think about what college is for,
what degrees are supposed to mean, what students are actually learning, and how
quickly the ground is shifting under them.

My own synthesis is that AI may push education back toward something closer to
classical learning: not in the nostalgic sense of copying the past, but in the
older goal of forming people who can reason across domains, communicate clearly,
recognize quality, adapt to change, and keep learning. In other words, AI
should make us less interested in funneling people into narrow task repetition
and more interested in developing adaptable polymath thinkers.

## Observation

The Teaching AI Fluency course put the 4D framework into motion for educators.
It framed AI Fluency as both descriptive and prescriptive: it maps what happens
when people work with AI, and it gives educators a structure for helping
students work with AI more effectively, efficiently, ethically, and safely.
This builds directly on my earlier note about the 4Ds as a common language for
human-AI collaboration: [The 4D Framework Is A Common Language For AI
Collaboration](2026-06-28-4d-framework-common-language.md).

The course organized teaching around practical questions:

- How do I introduce the framework to students?
- How do I assess whether students are developing real AI collaboration skills?
- How do I connect AI Fluency back to a discipline or field?

The most important move was that assessment was not just about the final
product. The course emphasized outcome-based, process-based, and
reflection-based assessment. That matters because AI can produce artifacts
quickly. The deeper learning is often in the student's choices, revisions,
tradeoffs, failures, recovery, reflection, and judgment.

That is where this started to connect with my older view of education.

## Why It Matters

Modern schooling often pushes people toward specialization early. Pick a major.
Pick a track. Learn the process. Repeat the process. Prove you can produce the
expected output.

That model made sense in a world where access to information was scarce and
many jobs depended on procedural expertise. But AI changes the shape of that
work. AI is increasingly good at process, repetition, first drafts, pattern
completion, summarization, classification, and routine production.

So the human edge moves somewhere else:

- knowing what is worth doing
- asking better questions
- combining ideas across fields
- judging quality
- seeing ethical and social consequences
- communicating intent
- adapting when the tools, constraints, or world change

That looks less like training people for one narrow lane and more like helping
people become intellectually flexible.

## Classical Learning As My Lens

The historical references here are mine. The Anthropic course inspired the
connection, but I am the one drawing the line from AI Fluency to classical
education.

Classical education has often been associated with the liberal arts tradition:
grammar, rhetoric, and logic in the trivium, and arithmetic, geometry, music,
and astronomy in the quadrivium. The point was not only job preparation. It was
formation: language, reasoning, argument, proportion, pattern, and judgment.

That is why old mathematical and philosophical problems still matter as
examples. Squaring the circle, for example, is not useful because people need
to square circles for a job. It matters because it trains exactness, proof,
limits, and intellectual humility. Platonic solids matter because they connect
geometry, philosophy, symmetry, aesthetics, and models of reality. These topics
are portals into disciplined thinking across multiple domains.

The Socrates line I had in mind is commonly attributed as: "I cannot teach
anybody anything. I can only make them think." I treat that as an educational
shorthand rather than a primary-source proof, because Socrates himself did not
leave writings. But the spirit is the point: the teacher is not just a
transmitter of answers. The teacher creates the conditions for thinking.

That feels newly relevant in the AI era.

## The Polymath Direction

The course helped me see AI Fluency as more than another technology skill. It
is a bridge into a different educational goal.

If AI can handle more narrow execution, then humans need to get better at
integration. We need people who can move between disciplines, translate ideas,
see analogies, evaluate tradeoffs, and keep their bearings when tools change.

That is the polymath idea I want to double down on. I do not mean every person
needs to become a genius in every field. I mean education should create people
who are capable of adaptation and change because they have practiced thinking
across boundaries.

AI Fluency fits that because the 4Ds are not tied to one discipline:

| 4D competency | Polymath habit it builds |
| --- | --- |
| Delegation | Understanding a problem well enough to decide what kind of work it is |
| Description | Translating goals, context, standards, and constraints clearly |
| Discernment | Recognizing quality, errors, gaps, and fit across outputs |
| Diligence | Acting with responsibility, transparency, and accountability |

Those are not just AI skills. They are change skills.

## What Teaching Looks Like Now

The course's assignment ideas pointed in the right direction. Students should
not only submit an artifact. They should show the collaboration that produced
it.

That can include:

- annotated chat logs
- learning journals
- process playbooks
- personal AI policy statements
- peer reviews of AI-assisted work
- comparisons across tools and approaches
- reflections on what changed between first attempt and final result

This is powerful because it makes invisible thinking visible. Students are not
only graded on whether AI helped them produce something polished. They are
asked to show how they delegated, described, discerned, revised, verified, and
took responsibility.

That is much closer to the kind of education I want to see more of. It values
judgment over output volume. It values reflection over hidden shortcuts. It
values process and growth over pretending the world has not changed.

## Evaluation Ideas

I can evaluate an AI-era learning experience with questions like:

- Does the assignment make student thinking visible, or only the final product?
- Does it assess outcome, process, and reflection?
- Does it teach students when to use AI, when not to, and why?
- Does it help students build discipline-specific quality criteria?
- Does it develop discernment instead of only rewarding polished output?
- Does it ask students to connect ideas across fields?
- Does it prepare students to adapt when tools, institutions, or careers
  change?
- Does it produce narrower task performers, or more capable human thinkers?

## Working Principle

AI should move education toward adaptable human thinkers: people who can reason
across disciplines, collaborate with machines, and use discernment when process
and repetition are no longer the scarce skills.
